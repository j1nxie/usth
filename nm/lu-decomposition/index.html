4. LU decomposition¶

4.1. Eigenvalues and eigenvectors¶

A homogeneous linear algebraic system has a right-hand side equal to zero.

[A][x]=0

Eigenvalue problems in engineering are typically of the below general form, with λ being the eigenvalue:

[[A]−λ[I]]{x}=0

For non-trivial solutions to be possible, the determinant of the matrix must equal zero.

|[A]−λ[I]|=0

Expanding the above determinant gives a polynomial in λ, called the characteristic polynomial. The roots of the polynomial are the solutions for the eigenvalues.

4.2. LU Decomposition¶

In simple terms, LU decomposition is another way of solving a system, breaking down the original matrix into two triangular matrices, lower and upper (hence, LU decomposition).

4.2.1. Formal definition¶

LU decomposition (where “LU” stands for “lower upper”, also known as LU factorization) factors a matrix as a product of a lower triangular matrix [L] and an upper triangular matrix [U].

[A]x=b↓[A]=[L][U]

4.2.2. Steps in LU factorization¶

Step 1: LU factorization:

[A] is factorized (or “decomposed”) into lower [L] and upper [U] triangular matrices.

[A]=[L][U]

Step 2: Substitution step:

[L] and [U] are used to determine a solution x for a right-hand side b. This step consists of two smaller steps:

  • Generate an intermediate vector d by forward substitution.

  • Substitute the result into equation which can be solved using backward subsitution for x.

4.2.3. Decomposing matrix A into L and U¶

[A]=[L][U]=[100l2110l31l321]=[u11u12u130u22u2300u33]

  • [U] is the same as the coefficient matrix at the end of forward elimination.

  • [L] is obtained using the multipliers that were used in forward elimination.

4.2.4. Finding the inverse of a square matrix¶

The inverse [B] of a square matrix [A] is defined as:

[A][B]=[I]=[B][A]

To find the inverse of a square matrix using LU decomposition, we start with assuming the first column of [B] to be [b11 b12 … bn1]T.

Using this and the definition of matrix multiplication, we obtain the first column of [B] as:

[A][b11b21…bn1]=[10…0]

Similarly, we also obtain the second column of [B] as:

[A][b12b22…bn2]=[01…0]

The remaining columns can be found in the same manner as above.

4.3. LU decomposition with pivoting¶

Partial pivoting is also necessary to obtain reliable solutions with LU factorizations. One way to do this involves using a permutation matrix, using the following steps.

Step 1: Elimination:

LU factorization with pivoting of a matrix [A] can be represented as:

[P][A]=[L][U]

[U] is generated by elimination with partial pivoting, while storing the multiplier factors in [L] and employing the permutation matrix [P] to keep track of row switches.

Step 2: Forward substitution:

[L] and [P] are used to perform elimination with pivot on b in order to generate the intermediate right-hand-side vector d.

[L]d=[P]b

Step 3: Back substitution:

Final solution is generated in the same fashion as Gauss elimination.

[U]x=d
